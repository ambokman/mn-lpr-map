▰ Minnesota Law: Minn. Stat. § 13.824

Minnesota limits LPR data retention to 60 days unless linked to a criminal investigation. Agencies must publish annual reports on LPR usage. Data may be shared with other agencies and, in some cases, private networks.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a license plate reader (LPR)?

A license plate reader is a camera system that uses optical character recognition to automatically read vehicle license plates. They can capture and timestamp the location of any vehicle passing the camera.

Are LPRs legal in Minnesota?

Yes. Minnesota law allows law enforcement to use LPRs but regulates data retention — agencies generally cannot retain data for more than 60 days unless linked to an active investigation.

What data is collected?

LPR systems typically record the license plate number, date, time, GPS location, and often a photo of the vehicle. Some systems share data across jurisdictions through national networks.

What is Flock Safety?

Flock Safety is a private company that sells LPR camera networks to police departments and HOAs. Several Minnesota cities have deployed Flock Safety “virtual fences” around their boundaries.

How was this data collected?

Locations are sourced from public records requests, ACLU of Minnesota filings, city council records, news reports, and government transparency disclosures. Some locations are approximate.

Is this data complete?

No — this map shows only known and documented LPR locations. Many more may exist. Mobile LPR units on patrol vehicles are not shown. Data may be outdated.
